Rotating Frames

This visualisation offers an overview of the behaviour of particles in rotating frames of reference, as presented in the year 1 Physics mechanics course.

Central to our understanding of rotating frames is idea that if a body rotates in the "lab frame", the rotating frame is the one in which the body is stationary. This is illustrated in the graphics to the left - the leftmost image shows the Earth's rotation in the "lab frame", and the one on the right shows how, in the Earth frame, the sun appears to rotate about the Earth.

Mathematically, it is useful to consider $\mathbf{r}$, the vector distance from the centre of the rotating body (the origin of the rotating frame). We also define a vector $\mathbf{\omega}$ to have the magnitude of the rotation rate (in radians per second) and the direction determined by applying the right hand rule to the rotation. We can now describe the graphics to the left mathematically: any point that is stationary in the rotating frame moves with a tangential speed of $\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{\omega}\times\mathbf{r}$.
